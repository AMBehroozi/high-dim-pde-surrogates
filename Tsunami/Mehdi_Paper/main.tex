\section*{Plain Language Summary}
AI models for predicting physical systems often need large amounts of training data, and this demand grows as problems become more complex. We address this challenge by teaching neural networks not only to predict outcomes, but also to learn how sensitive those outcomes are to changes in inputs. This makes each training example more informative, allowing accurate models to be built with less data while improving prediction, inversion, and generalization to unseen scenarios. The approach works across several physics problems, from benchmark transport and flow equations to large-scale tsunami modeling. In the tsunami case, sparse ocean gauge observations are used to reconstruct the seafloor deformation that triggered the wave and predict how the tsunami propagates. By combining physical sensitivity information with neural operators, this framework offers a faster and more data-efficient path toward physics-based forecasting and inverse modeling.


\section*{Key Points}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Enforcing physical sensitivities during training makes each sample more informative as input dimensionality grows, meaning less are needed.
    \item Achieves accurate forward prediction and inverse parameter recovery with significantly fewer training samples across diverse nonlinear PDEs.
    \item The framework reconstructs seafloor deformation from sparse buoy data and forecasts tsunamis at solver-level accuracy in under five minutes.
\end{itemize}
